NATO Allies Pledge to Spend More on Defense (No Really)

Since Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, NATO Allies have committed to spending more on defense. Pentagon hopes they are serious this time.

By Anthony D'Arrigo

June 26, 2022

NATO Secretary General Jens Stoltenberg (Francois Lenoir/Reuters)

2006 Commitment

In 2006, NATO Allies agreed to commit at least 2% of their Gross Domestic Product (GDP) to defense spending. At that time, less than one fifth (5 of 26) member countries dedicated more than 2%. In addition to defending the alliance, the 2% benchmark is an important indicator of a country’s political will to contribute to NATO’s common defense and protect the Alliance’s credibility.

By 2014, however, less NATO Allies met or exceeded their defense spending commitments, even though new countries had joined the alliance. As shown below, only three of 30—the US, UK, and Greece—honored the pledge.

2014 Recommittment

At the Wales Summit in 2014, in response to Russia’s illegal annexation of Crimea and turmoil and conflict in the Middle East, NATO Leaders recommitted to the 2% target. By 2021, 9 of 30 NATO Allies (30%) satisfied their 2% pledge; up from 10% in 2014 and 19% in 2006. Still, despite the marked increase, 70% of the alliance remained below the 2% benchmark.
